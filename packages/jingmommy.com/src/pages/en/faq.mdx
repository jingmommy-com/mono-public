#### Q: What is the difference between the Herbal Postpartum Meal and the Classic Postpartum Meal?

The menu items in both plans are identical, but the Herbal Postpartum Meal includes **one additional Chinese herbal tonic soup per day** to replenish blood, boost qi, eliminate dampness, remove stasis, and detoxify. Following traditional Chinese medicine principles, the formula progresses from mild nourishment to warm nourishment to deep replenishment — systematically building a strong foundation before intensive restoration. Since the postpartum period is the golden window for recovery, we recommend the Herbal plan (after all, you only go through this a few times in your life — investing in proper recovery pays off long-term). If you find you can't tolerate the herbal taste, switching to the Classic plan is always an option.

#### Q: Is there a difference between meals for vaginal delivery and C-section?

A: Although the delivery process differs, the postpartum meal content is the same for both. Both emphasize the same core wellness principles: replenishing qi and blood, warming the uterus, supporting digestion, avoiding cold foods, and promoting overall recovery.

- **Replenishing qi and blood:** Both types of delivery deplete the mother's energy and blood. Meals include ingredients like angelica root, red dates, pork liver, and eucommia bark to restore vitality and support blood production.
- **Warming the uterus:** Regardless of delivery method, the uterus needs special care. Meals include warming ingredients such as ginger and brown sugar to support uterine recovery and contraction.
- **Supporting digestion:** Postpartum digestion is weakened, so meals include easy-to-digest foods like glutinous rice, plain porridge, and millet porridge to aid digestive recovery.
- **Avoiding cold foods:** Cold-natured foods can suppress the body's yang energy. JingMommy carefully curates all meals to exclude raw, cold, and cooling ingredients.
- **Overall wellness:** Meals include beneficial herbs and ingredients such as goji berries, astragalus, and codonopsis to strengthen the immune system and support holistic recovery.

#### Q: Do I need to drink Sheng Hua Tang?

A: Sheng Hua Tang is a traditional Chinese herbal formula typically composed of angelica root, Sichuan lovage, motherwort, and peach seed. It works by promoting uterine contractions, speeding up the clearing of lochia, improving blood circulation, and nourishing blood. Whether you need it should be decided based on your individual constitution and your doctor's recommendation.

#### Q: I had gestational diabetes — will that affect my postpartum meals?

A: Gestational diabetes typically resolves on its own after delivery. We encourage you to consult your doctor to ensure your meal plan aligns with your health condition and nutritional needs.

#### Q: Why do the herbal tonics need to be taken for a full month?

A: A complete 30-day course is not only a time-honored tradition — it carries deep medical and psychological significance. JingMommy's herbal postpartum meals are carefully formulated to support the body's postpartum recovery over this period. The uterus and other reproductive organs need time to return to their pre-pregnancy state, and 30 days aligns naturally with the body's physiological recovery cycle, allowing a gradual and comprehensive restoration. Beyond the physical, the 30-day period also provides new mothers with a psychological buffer — helping them find balance in their new roles and daily rhythms.

#### Q: Are frozen meals still fresh?

A: Frozen food is a common and effective modern preservation method that maintains freshness to a significant degree. By lowering temperature, freezing dramatically slows bacterial growth and food spoilage, extending shelf life. When meals are flash-frozen, their nutrients, flavor, and texture are sealed in and largely preserved after thawing. Some foods may experience minor texture or taste changes, but overall freezing remains one of the best ways to preserve meal freshness with minimal nutritional loss.

---

#### Q: How do I reheat frozen meals?

A: Cut open the packaging and transfer the food to a microwave-safe or oven-safe container, then heat using a microwave or steamer. For convenience, we recommend moving the next day's meals from the freezer to the refrigerator the night before so they can thaw overnight and require less reheating time.
